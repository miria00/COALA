COALA: Convex Optimization for Alignment and Preference Learning on a Single GPU

Miria Feng, Mert Pilanci
Stanford University
ICML 2026
Paper Code BibTeX

Abstract

Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) to align with human preferences has driven the success of systems such as Gemini and ChatGPT. However, approaches like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) remain computationally expensive and complex. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) offers a simpler alternative but has limitations such as inconsistent ranking accuracy, high dependence on expensive GPU resources, and expansive hyperparameter tuning.

We propose the Convex Optimization for Alignment and Preference Learning Algorithm (COALA): a novel lightweight strategy with strong theoretical guarantees. By leveraging the convex optimization reformulation of neural networks, COALA eliminates the need for a reference model and obtains significant reduction in both training time and VRAM consumption, thus enabling efficient training on a single GPU.

Experiments across three datasets—including a 23,228-sample synthetic Educational Feedback dataset—and five models (including LLaMA-8B) demonstrate COALA's competitive performance and efficiency in utilizing as little as ≈17.6% of DPO's total TFLOPS. COALA exhibits stable, monotonically increasing rewards and reaches peak margins in significantly shorter time in comparison to traditional methods such as DPO and ORPO. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time convex optimization has been effectively applied to preference fine-tuning of LLMs.

Method

For a pre-trained backbone $f_{\theta_\mathrm{pre}}$, COALA stacks a convex two-layer ReLU network on the frozen features and trains it in two phases.

Phase I CRONOS — convex cvxNN training

Solve the convex reformulation of the two-layer ReLU network (Pilanci & Ergen, 2020) using CRONOS, an ADMM solver with preconditioned conjugate gradients (Feng, Frangella & Pilanci, 2024). The ADMM subproblems reduce to a vector add and one matrix–vector product, which JAX parallelises efficiently on a single GPU. Phase I outputs weights $(\Theta_1, \theta_2)$ with an $\mathcal{O}(1/K)$ ergodic convergence guarantee (Theorem 4.2).

Phase II Convex preference fine-tuning

Freeze $\Theta_1$. The reference-free COALA loss is convex in $\theta_2$:

$$ \min_{\theta_2}\;\; \mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l) \sim \mathcal{D}}\, \log\!\Big(1 + \exp\!\big(-\beta\, y_w\, \theta_2^\top (\Theta_1 f_{\theta_\mathrm{pre}}(x))_+ + \gamma\big)\Big). $$

This is a logistic regression in $\theta_2$, solved by AdamW (a stand-in for AGD) with an $\mathcal{O}(1/k^2)$ accelerated-gradient guarantee (Theorem 4.3). Because only the last layer is trained, COALA fits on a single RTX 4090 (24 GB) where DPO & ORPO require an A100 (40 GB).

Phase III Guided inference

At decoding time the frozen cvxNN head reweights the base LM's nucleus-sampled candidates (top-$p$ 0.9, top-$k$ 50) every $N = 5$ tokens. For each candidate $c$, the cvxNN scores the one-step extension $p_t \oplus c$, the scalar scores are min–max normalised, and a guidance scale $\lambda$ blends them into the base LM's distribution before sampling. No retraining — preference signal is applied at sampling time.

Results

Stable, monotonic reward margins

Across five backbones and three datasets, COALA's reward margin increases monotonically — a direct consequence of optimizing a convex objective. DPO's margins, in contrast, are noisy and frequently plateau or decay (paper Fig. 1).

EduFeedback dataset

We release EduFeedback: 26,621 GPT-4o-generated tutor conversations across 11 fields of study. Using the novel Alternating Population Strategy we expand these to 65,606 preference triplets without any external reward model — the first assistant turn becomes the "chosen" response and a later turn from the same conversation becomes the "rejected" response. This exploits the natural drift from direct answers to tangential follow-ups in objective domain dialogs.

COALA trained on these pairs achieves a 39.1% win rate in human evaluation, suggesting the strategy captures genuine preference signal at scale. The dataset is released alongside the paper at the project repository.
